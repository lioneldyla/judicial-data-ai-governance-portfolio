## Executive Summary

This document defines the ten-year research strategy for the **Data Governance for Intelligent Justice Systems** programme. It articulates strategic objectives, research priorities, methodological orientations, expected outputs, and milestone timelines for the period 2026–2035. The strategy is designed to support doctoral research, publications in international journals, policy engagement, and the construction of an internationally recognised research infrastructure on judicial data governance in Africa.

---

## 1. Strategic Context

### 1.1 The Problem Space

Justice systems across sub-Saharan Africa face converging pressures:

- **Data deficits**: fragmented, incomplete, or unreliable judicial statistics;
- **Governance gaps**: absence of data governance frameworks adapted to judicial institutions;
- **AI exposure**: increasing deployment of algorithmic tools without adequate regulatory frameworks;
- **Capacity constraints**: limited human and technical resources for digital transformation;
- **Trust deficits**: erosion of public confidence in judicial institutions and data quality.

### 1.2 The Opportunity

A window of opportunity exists to shape the governance of judicial AI and data systems before they become entrenched. Early institutional intervention, supported by original research, can prevent the replication of governance failures documented in other sectors and regions.

## 3. Research Priorities

### Priority 1 — Judicial Data Governance Framework (JDGF)

Develop and validate a comprehensive governance framework for judicial data systems, grounded in institutional theory, FAIR principles, and African legal-administrative contexts.

### Priority 2 — AI Regulation in Justice Systems

Analyse existing and emerging AI regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act, national AI strategies, OHADA developments) and their applicability to judicial AI deployment in Africa.

### Priority 3 — Judicial Statistics and Open Data

Investigate the production, quality, publication, and use of judicial statistics as a component of democratic accountability and evidence-based justice policy.

### Priority 4 — Digital Transformation Governance

Examine institutional governance models for the digital transformation of justice systems, with attention to change management, capacity building, and technology procurement.

### Priority 5 — Comparative African Justice Systems

Develop comparative analyses of judicial data governance across OHADA member states, identifying commonalities, divergences, and transferable good practices.

## 4. Methodological Orientations

- **Institutional analysis**: drawing on neo-institutional theory, public administration, and organisational sociology
- **Comparative law**: OHADA framework, national legislation, regional regulatory instruments
- **Mixed methods**: qualitative case studies combined with quantitative judicial statistics analysis
- **Participatory approaches**: engagement with judicial practitioners, registry staff, and data professionals
- **Design science**: development of frameworks and tools validated through iterative testing

## 6. Sustainability and Open Science

All outputs shall be deposited in Zenodo with persistent DOIs. The GitHub repository shall be maintained as the primary open-science infrastructure. ORCID, SSRN, and OpenAIRE shall be used for academic dissemination. The strategy is designed to remain operational without dependence on any single institution or funding source.
