## 1. Collaboration principles

The Lab welcomes collaboration under five principles.

1. **Scientific rigour.** Every collaboration produces or supports outputs
   that meet the Lab's [Scientific Quality standards](../LAB_CHARTER.md#32-quality-standards)
   — transparent methodology, reproducible analysis, honest
   acknowledgement of uncertainty.
2. **Open science.** Collaborative outputs adhere to the
   [FAIR principles](https://www.go-fair.org/) and are deposited under
   open licenses (CC BY 4.0 for writing, MIT / Apache 2.0 for software)
   unless a specific legal or ethical constraint applies.
3. **Contextual relevance.** Collaborations that centre African
   institutional realities — rather than transplanting frameworks
   designed elsewhere — are prioritized.
4. **Ethical integrity.** No collaboration operates on data or in
   settings that would violate [`../RESEARCH_ETHICS.md`](../RESEARCH_ETHICS.md).
5. **Attribution.** Authorship, data ownership, and publication rights
   are agreed in writing at the outset of every collaboration.

*Source of authority: [`../LAB_CHARTER.md`](../LAB_CHARTER.md) §3, §7.*

## 2. Contribution process

### 2.1 For researchers and practitioners

Contributions typically fall into one of the following:

- **Case-study contribution** — sharing evidence, documents, or field
  observations that ground a case study.
- **Comparative contribution** — bringing a comparator country / institution.
- **Methodological contribution** — critiquing or extending a framework
  (JDGF, SPJEJ) in preparation.
- **Data contribution** — sharing datasets under compatible licensing.
- **Editorial contribution** — reviewing draft working papers.

Process:

1. Open an issue at
   <https://github.com/lioneldyla/judicial-data-ai-governance-portfolio/issues>
   describing the intended contribution.
2. Agree on scope, authorship expectations, licensing.
3. Submit contribution as a pull request (for text/data) or file
   directly on the case study issue.

Full contribution guidelines: [`../CONTRIBUTING.md`](../CONTRIBUTING.md).

### 2.2 For institutions

Institutional engagement is typically:

- **Case-study partnership** — the institution is a subject of study
  (with informed consent, data-sharing agreements, and
  publication-review arrangements).
- **Advisory partnership** — the institution provides expert review of
  frameworks or working papers.
- **Adoption partnership** — the institution adopts a framework or tool
  and documents its implementation experience.

Institutional partnerships are governed by written agreements specifying
data-sharing terms, publication rights, and expected time commitments.

## 6. Ways to signal interest

If you are reading this and want to collaborate:

- **Researcher or practitioner** — open a GitHub issue with subject
  `[COLLAB]` describing your context, proposed contribution, and
  expectations.
- **Institution** — email or LinkedIn the researcher directly with the
  same information.
- **Journal editor / conference organizer** — direct email is fastest.

The Lab commits to responding to serious inquiries within four weeks.

---

## 7. What the Lab does NOT do

To keep expectations calibrated:

- The Lab does not provide paid consulting services.
- The Lab does not provide legal advice or judicial expert testimony.
- The Lab does not endorse specific vendors or commercial products.
- The Lab does not conduct research on individuals' judicial data
  without appropriate ethical clearances.

## 8. Attribution and authorship

Every collaboration that produces a scholarly output records author
contributions using the [CRediT taxonomy](https://credit.niso.org/).
See [`../working_papers/WP01/AUTHORS.md`](../working_papers/WP01/AUTHORS.md)
for the taxonomy in use.

Collaborators are credited at their level of substantive contribution.
Symbolic authorship (adding names for institutional convenience) is not
practised.
