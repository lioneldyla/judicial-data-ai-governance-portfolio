## Preamble

Justice systems are among the most critical public institutions in any democratic society. They protect rights, resolve disputes, enforce accountability, and provide the legal foundations upon which social trust is built. Yet across much of the African continent, justice institutions face persistent challenges: structural data deficits, fragmented information ecosystems, insufficient governance frameworks, and growing pressure to integrate artificial intelligence without adequate regulatory preparation.

This manifesto articulates the foundational commitments, scientific principles, and ethical imperatives that guide the **Data Governance for Intelligent Justice Systems** research programme.

---

## I. Core Conviction

The digital transformation of justice is not merely a technological challenge. It is a governance challenge, an institutional challenge, and a human rights challenge.

Data without governance is risk. Artificial intelligence without accountability is danger. Digital infrastructure without institutional capacity is illusion.

This research programme exists to bridge the gap between technological possibility and institutional reality — to build the evidence base, frameworks, and tools that enable justice systems to govern their data responsibly, adopt AI ethically, and serve citizens effectively.

---

## II. Research Commitments

### 2.1 Scientific Rigour

All research produced within this programme shall meet international standards of scientific quality: transparent methodology, reproducible analysis, peer review, and honest acknowledgement of uncertainty and limitation.

### 2.2 Open Science

All research outputs shall adhere to FAIR principles (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable). Working papers, frameworks, datasets, and software shall be made publicly available under open licenses wherever legally and ethically permissible.

### 2.3 Contextual Relevance

Research shall be grounded in the institutional realities of African justice systems. Generic transplantation of frameworks designed for high-income contexts is insufficient. Every framework, model, or recommendation produced shall be validated against the specific legal, institutional, and resource conditions of the target environment.

### 2.4 Interdisciplinarity

Judicial data governance requires legal analysis, public administration theory, data science, organisational sociology, and AI ethics. This programme maintains a deliberately interdisciplinary posture, refusing the reductionism of any single discipline.

### 2.5 Practice-Orientation

Theory without practice is incomplete. Every research output shall be designed with usability in mind: frameworks shall be implementable, indicators shall be measurable, recommendations shall be actionable by institutional actors with real-world constraints.

---

## III. Ethical Imperatives

### 3.1 Human Dignity

The rights and dignity of individuals who interact with justice systems — as parties, witnesses, victims, or accused — shall be central to every research question, framework, and recommendation.

### 3.2 Institutional Accountability

This programme does not study justice systems as neutral objects. It investigates them as institutions of power with responsibilities toward the public. Accountability — of institutions, of data systems, and of AI — is a core analytical lens.

### 3.3 Research Integrity

No research output shall be produced, modified, or suppressed to serve political, institutional, or personal interests. Intellectual honesty is non-negotiable.

### 3.4 Positionality

The primary researcher is a practitioner within the Ivorian justice system. This positionality is a scientific asset — it provides access, contextual knowledge, and practical credibility — and a potential limitation that requires ongoing critical reflexivity.

---

## IV. Vision

A future in which every African justice system:

- governs its data as a strategic public asset;
- adopts artificial intelligence within accountable, rights-respecting frameworks;
- produces reliable, accessible judicial statistics that inform policy and public trust;
- trains its personnel to understand, challenge, and govern digital systems;
- contributes to regional and global knowledge on digital justice governance.

---

## V. Commitment to Africa

This research programme is explicitly positioned as a contribution to African knowledge production. It refuses the posture of a peripheral institution absorbing frameworks designed elsewhere. It aspires to generate original theoretical and empirical contributions that speak to African institutional realities and that participate in global scientific conversations on equal footing.
