## 1. Statement of Ethical Commitment

The **Data Governance for Intelligent Justice Systems** research programme is grounded in the conviction that research about justice systems must itself be just. This means conducting research with the same rigour, impartiality, transparency, and respect for human dignity that we advocate for in the institutions we study.

This document establishes the ethical framework for all research activities associated with this programme. It draws from international research ethics standards while recognizing the specific ethical challenges that arise in research involving judicial data, vulnerable populations, public institutions, and artificial intelligence systems.

---

## 2. Core Ethical Principles

### 2.1 Respect for Persons

- All research involving human subjects is conducted with their free, prior, and informed consent
- Participant anonymity and confidentiality are protected unless consent to identification is explicitly granted
- Special protections apply to vulnerable groups (victims, minors, persons under judicial proceedings)

### 2.2 Beneficence and Non-Maleficence

- Research is designed to produce knowledge that benefits justice systems and the people they serve
- Foreseeable harms — including harms from data publication, algorithm bias, or institutional disruption — are assessed and mitigated before research begins
- The programme does not conduct research whose primary anticipated benefit is commercial

### 2.3 Justice and Equity

- Research benefits are not concentrated in high-income countries or elite institutions; African justice systems are not only research subjects — they are knowledge co-producers
- Authorship, credit, and recognition are distributed fairly among all contributors
- Research does not reinforce colonial or extractive dynamics in knowledge production

### 2.4 Scientific Integrity

- Data is not fabricated, falsified, or selectively reported
- Methodology is described with sufficient transparency for independent replication
- Conflicts of interest — institutional, financial, or personal — are declared
- Negative results are reported

### 2.5 Responsible AI Research

- AI systems studied or developed in this programme are evaluated for bias, fairness, and potential for discrimination
- No AI system is proposed for deployment in judicial contexts without explicit human oversight mechanisms
- Explainability is treated as a prerequisite for judicial AI applications, not an optional feature

## 3. Judicial Data Ethics

### 3.1 Sensitivity Classification

Judicial data is classified into three sensitivity levels:

| Level | Description | Publication Protocol |
|-------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Level 1** | Aggregate, anonymized statistical data | Open publication permitted |
| **Level 2** | Institutional process data (non-personal) | Open publication with methodology declaration |
| **Level 3** | Individual case, party, or personal data | Not published; access via controlled protocol only |

### 3.2 Anonymization Standards

All Level 3 data used in research is anonymized before analysis. Anonymization procedures are documented. Re-identification risk is assessed before any publication involving microdata.

### 3.3 Institutional Data Agreements

Access to judicial data held by the Cour de Cassation of Côte d'Ivoire or other institutions is governed by formal data sharing agreements. These agreements are referenced in relevant research outputs and available to reviewers upon request.

## 4. AI Ethics in Research Design

Research involving the development or evaluation of AI systems for judicial use follows the **UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (2021)** and the following principles:

1. **Human oversight**: AI systems cannot replace human judgment in judicial decisions
2. **Non-discrimination**: Algorithmic outputs are audited for protected characteristic disparities
3. **Transparency**: The architecture, training data, and decision logic of studied AI systems are documented
4. **Accountability**: Responsibility chains for AI system outcomes are clearly defined
5. **Privacy**: AI systems handling personal judicial data are privacy-by-design compliant

---

## 5. Publication Ethics

### 5.1 Authorship

Authorship practices follow the ICMJE Recommendations as defined in `PUBLICATION_POLICY.md`. Gift authorship, ghost authorship, and coercive citation are prohibited.

### 5.2 Plagiarism and Originality

All publications represent original work. Quotations are attributed. Paraphrase of prior work is distinguished from original analysis. Appropriate citation is given for all ideas, data, methods, and frameworks drawn from existing literature.

### 5.3 Corrections and Retractions

Errors discovered after publication are corrected promptly. Corrections are documented in CHANGELOG.md. Retractions, if ever necessary, follow journal or repository retraction protocols.

## 6. Conflict of Interest Declaration

The researcher declares the following affiliations that could constitute a conflict of interest in some research contexts:

- Employment by the judiciary of Côte d'Ivoire (Cour de Cassation / INFJ)
- Research topics directly related to the researcher's professional responsibilities

These affiliations are declared in all relevant publications. Research conclusions are not derived from internal institutional data unless proper access protocols have been followed.
