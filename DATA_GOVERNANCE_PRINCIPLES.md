## 1. Purpose

This document articulates the foundational principles of data governance as applied to judicial institutions and justice systems. It constitutes a normative reference for the research programme and provides the theoretical underpinning for all governance frameworks developed herein, including the Judicial Data Governance Framework (JDGF).

---

## 2. Definition of Data Governance

For the purposes of this programme, **data governance** is defined as:

> The system of rights, responsibilities, policies, standards, and processes by which an organization ensures that its data assets are managed, protected, and leveraged in a manner consistent with its mission, legal obligations, and stakeholder expectations.

In judicial contexts, data governance encompasses the full lifecycle of judicial data — from generation through court proceedings, to storage, analysis, dissemination, and archival — while ensuring compliance with due process, privacy rights, and institutional mandates.

## 3. Core Principles

### 3.1 Principle of Accountability

Judicial data is public-trust data. Institutions holding judicial data are accountable to citizens, parties, legal practitioners, and oversight bodies for how that data is generated, managed, and used. Accountability requires clear ownership, documented processes, and accessible audit trails.

### 3.2 Principle of Data Quality

Decisions affecting the rights of individuals may be influenced by judicial statistics, caseload analyses, and algorithmic tools. The quality — accuracy, completeness, consistency, timeliness — of judicial data is therefore a matter of justice, not only of management.

### 3.3 Principle of Privacy by Design

Data governance frameworks for judicial institutions must embed privacy protection from the architecture of data systems, not as an afterthought. Personal data arising from judicial proceedings is among the most sensitive that institutions hold.

### 3.4 Principle of Interoperability

Disjointed data ecosystems within national justice systems undermine evidence-based policy, cross-institutional coordination, and the rights of parties. Judicial data governance must work toward semantic, syntactic, and organizational interoperability across institutions.

### 3.5 Principle of Openness with Proportionality

Judicial data serves multiple publics. Aggregate and anonymized data should be open to support transparency and democratic accountability. Personal data and data that could identify parties must be protected. The principle of proportionality governs the trade-off between openness and protection.

### 3.6 Principle of Data Sovereignty

African justice institutions should control the architecture, standards, and governance of their own judicial data infrastructure. External dependencies — technical or regulatory — that compromise institutional sovereignty over judicial data are inconsistent with good governance.

### 3.7 Principle of Ethical Use

Data generated by judicial proceedings exists in service of justice. Its use — including for statistical analysis, machine learning, or policy research — must remain subordinate to justice values: fairness, dignity, non-discrimination, and due process.

### 3.8 Principle of Evidence-Based Governance

Judicial governance decisions — including resource allocation, procedural reform, and policy design — should be informed by systematic analysis of judicial data. Evidence-based governance requires both high-quality data and the institutional capacity to analyze and use it.

### 3.9 Principle of Participation

Data governance frameworks for justice systems should be developed in consultation with all stakeholders: judges, clerks, lawyers, civil society, and, where feasible, court users. Participatory design produces frameworks that are more legitimate, more functional, and more durable.

### 3.10 Principle of Proportionality in Automation

Automated data processing in judicial contexts — including AI-assisted analysis — must be proportionate to the importance and sensitivity of the decision supported. Higher-stakes decisions require stronger human oversight, more rigorous validation, and more explicit accountability.

## 4. Data Governance Framework Dimensions

Governance frameworks for judicial data should address the following dimensions:

| Dimension | Key Questions |
|-----------|---------------|
| **Authority** | Who owns data? Who decides on access? |
| **Policy** | What rules govern data creation, use, and deletion? |
| **Standards** | What formats, vocabularies, and quality levels apply? |
| **Architecture** | How is data stored, integrated, and secured? |
| **Processes** | How are data lifecycle activities managed? |
| **Roles** | Who is responsible for what data activities? |
| **Technology** | What systems and tools support governance? |
| **Culture** | How do institutional actors relate to data? |

## 5. African Context Considerations

The application of data governance principles to African justice systems requires attention to context-specific factors:

1. **Resource constraints.** Many African justice institutions operate with limited budgets, technology infrastructure, and trained personnel. Governance frameworks must be designed for progressive implementation.
2. **Legal pluralism.** Many African jurisdictions combine statutory law, customary law, and religious law. Data governance must accommodate legally plural judicial systems.
3. **Colonial data legacies.** Historical judicial data in many African countries reflects colonial-era legal structures and categorizations. Governance of this data requires critical historical awareness.
4. **Sovereignty and digital infrastructure.** Questions of cloud hosting, technology vendor dependence, and cross-border data flows are particularly acute for national justice institutions.
5. **OHADA harmonization.** For member states of the OHADA Treaty (Organisation pour l'Harmonisation en Afrique du Droit des Affaires), data governance frameworks must align with harmonized commercial law requirements.
